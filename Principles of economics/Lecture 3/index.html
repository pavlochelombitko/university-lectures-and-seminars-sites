Section 1 — Foundations

What Is Economic Growth?

The core concept and how it differs from business cycle recovery.

📈 Definition

Economic growth is a sustained increase in real GDP per person over time. At a deeper level, it represents a permanent outward shift of the economy's production possibilities frontier (PPF).

It reflects three things: expansion of productive capacity, improvements in efficiency, and structural transformation of the economy.

🎯 Growth is a long-run supply-side phenomenon. It concerns the evolution of potential output Y*, not short-run demand fluctuations.

🔀 Growth vs. Business Cycle

Real GDP can increase for two very different reasons. Only one counts as true economic growth:

Cyclical Recovery

Economy moves back toward full employment. The output gap (Y − Y*) closes. This is temporary — moving from inside the PPF back to the frontier.

True Economic Growth

Potential GDP (Y*) itself increases. The PPF shifts outward. This is permanent — the economy can now produce more than before.

💡 Recovery restores lost output. Growth raises future income permanently. This distinction is crucial for evaluating policy.

🔄 What Shifts the PPF Outward?

Four sources drive the permanent expansion of productive capacity:

  • Capital accumulation — more machines, factories, infrastructure
  • Human capital — better education, skills, and training
  • Technological innovation — new ways to produce more with less
  • Institutional improvements — stronger property rights, rule of law, governance
Section 2 — Measurement

Measuring Growth

Three methods to quantify growth, the power of compounding, and the Rule of 70.

📏 Three Ways to Measure

1. Absolute Change — the simplest measure:

Absolute ChangeΔY = Yₜ − Yₜ₋₁

Useful for scale effects, but misleading when comparing countries of different sizes.

2. Growth Rate — captures proportional change:

Growth Rateg = (Yₜ − Yₜ₋₁) / Yₜ₋₁ × 100

This is the standard measure economists use. A 3% growth rate means the economy produced 3% more real output than the previous year.

3. Real GDP per Person — the living standards measure:

Per Capita GDPReal GDP per capita = Real GDP / Population

Living standards rise only if GDP growth exceeds population growth:

Condition for Rising Living Standardsg_Y > g_Population
⚠️ Per capita GDP measures average income — it says nothing about distribution, welfare, or sustainability.

📐 The Power of Compound Growth

Growth compounds exponentially — small differences in growth rates create massive long-run divergence:

Compound GrowthYₜ = Y₀ × (1 + g)ᵗ

Example: Two countries start with $10,000 GDP per capita. Country A grows at 1%, Country B at 3%. After 100 years: A has ~$27,000 while B has ~$192,000 — a 7× gap from just 2 percentage points.

7️⃣ The Rule of 70

A quick shortcut to estimate how long it takes for GDP to double:

Rule of 70Years to double = 70 / g

Section 3 — Sources

Engines of Growth

The two fundamental sources, and the four drivers of productivity.

⚙️ Two Engines

All growth ultimately decomposes into just two sources:

Output IdentityY = Labor Hours × Output per Hour
In Growth Ratesg_Y = g_Labor + g_Productivity

Engine 1: More Labor

Population growth, higher participation, more hours. But diminishing MPL means total GDP rises while GDP per worker may fall.

Limitation Population growth alone doesn't guarantee higher living standards.

Engine 2: Higher Productivity

More output per hour worked. This is the real engine of prosperity — it drives real wages and living standards.

Key fact In competitive markets: Real Wage = MPL.

⚡ Labor Productivity

DefinitionProductivity = Real GDP / Total Labor Hours

In competitive markets, Real Wage = MPL. So productivity growth directly drives wage growth and living standards.

🚀 Four Drivers of Productivity

1. Physical Capital Accumulation Level Effect

Capital deepening (K/L↑) raises output per worker. However, diminishing marginal returns limit the long-run effect.

2. Human Capital Formation Growth Effect

Education and skills increase effective labor. Human capital enhances adaptability, facilitates innovation, and improves institutions.

With Human CapitalY = AKβ(HL)α — H = human capital per worker

3. Technological Progress (TFP) Growth Effect

Technology increases Total Factor Productivity (A). Knowledge is partially non-rival and generates spillovers — weakening diminishing returns.

4. Institutions Growth Effect

Property rights, rule of law, governance — encourage investment, reduce uncertainty, support innovation. Institutional quality strongly correlates with long-run income.

The Production Function

Cobb-Douglas and growth accounting — the mathematical backbone.

🏭 Aggregate Production Function

Usually α + β = 1 (constant returns to scale).

📊 Growth Accounting

Growth Accounting Equationg_Y = g_A + α·g_L + β·g_K
  • g_A = TFP growth (the "Solow residual")
  • α·g_L = contribution of labor growth
  • β·g_K = contribution of capital accumulation
📌 Empirical insight: In advanced economies, the largest share of growth comes from TFP — not just piling up more labor or capital. Technology is the key driver.
Section 5 — Theory

Three Growth Theories

Classical, Neoclassical, and Endogenous — how economists explain long-run growth.

Classical Growth Theory

Thomas Robert Malthus (1766–1834)
Income ↑→ Population ↑→ Diminishing returns→ Income ↓

Prediction: Long-run stagnation at subsistence. Was historically relevant before industrialization — technological progress eventually broke the Malthusian trap.

Neoclassical Growth Theory

Robert Solow (1956)
Savings→ Investment→ Capital ↑→ Diminishing returns→ Steady state

Technology is exogenous. The economy converges to a steady state. Higher savings raises income level but not the long-run growth rate. Only technology keeps growth going permanently.

Endogenous (New) Growth Theory

Paul Romer & Robert Lucas (1980s–1990s)

Technology is endogenous — produced through deliberate R&D and education. Ideas are non-rival, partially excludable, and generate spillovers. Result: growth can persist without diminishing returns, and policies can permanently raise growth rates.

Section 6 — Applications

Policy Implications

What can governments do? Level effects vs. growth effects.

🎯 Level Effects vs. Growth Effects

Level Effect

One-time upward shift in income. GDP jumps higher but then grows at the same rate. Example: increasing savings.

Growth Effect

Permanent increase in the growth rate. GDP grows faster indefinitely. Example: R&D subsidies.

💰 Stimulate Savings

Higher saving → higher capital stock → higher income level. Does not permanently raise growth rate (Solow). In global capital markets, domestic savings don't automatically become domestic investment.

Level Effect

🔬 Invest in R&D

Innovation creates positive externalities — firms underinvest because they can't capture all spillover benefits. Public support corrects this market failure. Potential permanent growth effects under endogenous theory.

Growth Effect

🎓 Improve Education

Builds human capital, innovation capacity, and technology adoption ability. In endogenous models, human capital accumulation directly sustains growth.

Growth Effect

🌍 Promote Trade

Expands market size, drives specialization, diffuses technology, creates competitive pressure. Open economies show higher productivity growth. Trade supports knowledge spillovers.

Both Effects

🤝 International Aid

Evidence is mixed. Effective only with strong institutions, good governance, and credible policies. Without institutional reform, capital inflows may be wasted.

Conditional

🏆 The Core Message: Long-run prosperity depends on productivity growth — driven by technology, human capital, and institutions. Small differences in growth rates create massive gaps over time (Rule of 70).
